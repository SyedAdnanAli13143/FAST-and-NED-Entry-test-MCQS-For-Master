# Essay Writing (10% of your score)

Graded mainly on **structure, grammar, and clarity of argument** — not on technical accuracy. A well-organized, grammatically clean essay on a simple idea beats a brilliant idea written in a messy paragraph.

## 1. The structure to memorize (use it for ANY topic)

1. **Introduction (2–3 sentences):** Restate the topic in your own words + state your main position/thesis.
2. **Body Paragraph 1:** First main point + one concrete example.
3. **Body Paragraph 2:** Second main point + one concrete example.
4. **Body Paragraph 3 (optional, if time allows):** Counter-argument or a third point.
5. **Conclusion (2–3 sentences):** Restate your thesis in different words + a forward-looking closing line.

Target length given limited test time: **180–280 words** (5 paragraphs, roughly 4–6 sentences each). Don't aim for an epic — aim for *complete and clean*.

## 2. Time budget (assume ~20–25 minutes for the essay)

- 2 min: pick a clear position, jot 3 bullet points on scratch paper (don't start writing blind).
- 15–18 min: write.
- 3–5 min: re-read once for grammar, spelling, and missing words. This single pass catches most careless errors.

## 3. Likely topic categories

FAST essay prompts are usually general-knowledge / opinion topics, not technical ones. Practice writing on at least one topic from each category so you're never starting cold:

- **Technology & Society:** "Impact of Artificial Intelligence on jobs", "Role of social media in society", "Is technology making us more or less connected?"
- **Education & Career:** "Why I chose this field of study", "Importance of higher education", "Should education be free for everyone?"
- **Social Issues (Pakistan-relevant):** "Problems faced by youth in Pakistan", "Importance of skill development", "Brain drain — a curse or a chance?"
- **Environment:** "Climate change and what individuals can do", "Importance of clean water"
- **Abstract/General:** "Hard work vs smart work", "Failure is the key to success", "Time management"

> Since you're applying for **AI / Data Science**, also prepare one essay specifically tying your motivation to the field — examiners often like to see coherent, personal reasoning, and you'll write it more naturally than a topic you have no opinion on.

## 4. Sample Essay 1 — "The Role of Artificial Intelligence in Society" (~230 words)

> Artificial Intelligence (AI) has moved from research labs into everyday life faster than almost any technology before it. Today, AI recommends what we watch, helps doctors detect diseases earlier, and powers the voice assistants in our phones. This rapid adoption brings both opportunity and responsibility.
>
> The biggest advantage of AI is efficiency. Tasks that once took humans hours — sorting data, scanning medical images, translating languages — can now be done in seconds, freeing people to focus on more creative and judgment-based work. In fields like healthcare and agriculture, AI-driven predictions are already helping professionals make faster, better-informed decisions, which matters enormously in a country like Pakistan where skilled professionals are stretched thin.
>
> However, this power comes with real concerns. AI systems can inherit biases from the data they are trained on, and the automation of routine jobs raises legitimate questions about employment for less-skilled workers. Society cannot simply adopt AI blindly; it must also invest in regulation, transparency, and re-skilling programs so that the benefits of this technology are shared broadly rather than concentrated among a few.
>
> In conclusion, Artificial Intelligence is neither a miracle nor a threat by itself — it is a tool whose impact depends entirely on how thoughtfully we apply it. As someone pursuing further studies in this field, I see my own responsibility not just in building intelligent systems, but in building them responsibly.

## 5. Sample Essay 2 — "Hard Work vs Smart Work" (~210 words)

> For generations, hard work has been praised as the only honest path to success. But in a fast-changing world, the debate between hard work and smart work has become increasingly relevant. The truth is that the two are not opposites — they are partners.
>
> Hard work builds discipline and depth. A student who studies consistently, or a professional who puts in long hours mastering their craft, develops a foundation of knowledge that cannot be skipped. Without this base, "working smart" has nothing to build on.
>
> Smart work, on the other hand, is about using that foundation efficiently — prioritizing the right tasks, using better tools, and not repeating the same mistakes. Someone who studies eight hours without a plan often achieves less than someone who studies four hours with a clear strategy and full focus.
>
> The most successful people combine both: the discipline to put in real effort, and the wisdom to direct that effort where it matters most. In conclusion, the real question is not "hard work or smart work" but how to apply hard work *smartly* — with a clear goal, the right method, and the persistence to see it through.

## 6. Grammar checklist — re-read your essay for these specifically

- [ ] Subject-verb agreement ("he writes", not "he write")
- [ ] Consistent tense throughout (don't drift from past to present mid-paragraph)
- [ ] No sentence fragments — every sentence has a subject and a verb
- [ ] Articles used correctly (a/an/the) — common slip for non-native English writers
- [ ] No repeated words right next to each other (re-read slowly, this is the #1 careless error under time pressure)
- [ ] Paragraph breaks are present — a single unbroken block of text looks disorganized even if the content is fine

## 7. Self-grading rubric (use this after each practice essay)

| Criterion | What to check |
|---|---|
| Structure | Clear intro, body, conclusion — visible at a glance |
| Argument | One clear position, not several unrelated ideas |
| Examples | At least one concrete example per main point |
| Grammar | Re-read pass done, no obvious errors |
| Length | 180–280 words — not a one-paragraph answer, not a wall of text |
