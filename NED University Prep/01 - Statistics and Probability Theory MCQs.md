# Statistics & Probability — Theory (~20 of the ~30 Stats/Probability questions)

This is your single highest-value subject — worth more marks than every other NED topic combined. File `08` covers a few extra named tests (Regression, ANOVA, T-Tests, Chi-Square) that extend this same subject.

## A. Types of Data & Levels of Measurement

**Q1.** Data that can only take whole, countable values (e.g. number of students in a class) is called:
A) Continuous data  B) Discrete data  C) Qualitative data  D) Ordinal data
**Answer: B) Discrete data**

**Q2.** Data that can take any value within a range (e.g. height, weight, time) is called:
A) Discrete data  B) Continuous data  C) Nominal data  D) Categorical data
**Answer: B) Continuous data**

**Q3.** Which level of measurement has categories with **no inherent order** (e.g. gender, blood group, city of birth)?
A) Nominal  B) Ordinal  C) Interval  D) Ratio
**Answer: A) Nominal**

**Q4.** Which level of measurement has **ordered** categories but no fixed/equal distance between values (e.g. satisfaction: low/medium/high)?
A) Nominal  B) Ordinal  C) Interval  D) Ratio
**Answer: B) Ordinal**

**Q5.** Which level of measurement has equal intervals **and** a true zero point (e.g. weight, height, income — "zero" really means "none")?
A) Nominal  B) Ordinal  C) Interval  D) Ratio
**Answer: D) Ratio**

**Q6.** Temperature in Celsius has equal intervals but **no true zero** (0°C doesn't mean "no temperature"). This makes it which scale?
A) Nominal  B) Ordinal  C) Interval  D) Ratio
**Answer: C) Interval**

## B. Central Tendency & Dispersion

**Q7.** Which measure of central tendency is most affected/distorted by extreme outliers?
A) Mean  B) Median  C) Mode  D) None of these
**Answer: A) Mean**

**Q8.** Which measure of central tendency is preferred for skewed data with outliers (e.g. income data)?
A) Mean  B) Median  C) Mode  D) Range
**Answer: B) Median**

**Q9.** The value that occurs **most frequently** in a dataset is the:
A) Mean  B) Median  C) Mode  D) Range
**Answer: C) Mode**

**Q10.** "Variance" measures:
A) The central value of the data  B) How spread out the data is from the mean  C) The most common value  D) The total count of data points
**Answer: B)**

**Q11.** Standard deviation is:
A) The square of the variance  B) The square root of the variance  C) The same as the mean  D) The same as the range
**Answer: B)**

**Q12.** A standard deviation of zero means:
A) All values in the dataset are different  B) All values in the dataset are identical  C) The mean is negative  D) The data is perfectly normal
**Answer: B)**

## C. Population, Samples & Sampling Bias

**Q13.** A "population" in statistics refers to:
A) A small, randomly chosen group  B) The entire group being studied  C) Only people, never objects  D) A normally distributed dataset
**Answer: B)**

**Q14.** A "sample" is:
A) The entire population  B) A subset of the population used to draw conclusions about the whole  C) Always a biased group  D) Always exactly 50% of the population
**Answer: B)**

**Q15.** A sampling method where every member of the population has an **equal chance** of being chosen is called:
A) Convenience sampling  B) Simple random sampling  C) Biased sampling  D) Voluntary sampling
**Answer: B)**

**Q16.** "Sampling bias" occurs when:
A) The sample size is large  B) The sample does not represent the population fairly  C) Data is normally distributed  D) Mean equals median
**Answer: B)**

## D. Distributions

**Q17.** A Normal Distribution curve is:
A) Always skewed left  B) Always skewed right  C) Symmetric and bell-shaped  D) Always flat/uniform
**Answer: C)**

**Q18.** In a perfectly normal distribution, the mean, median, and mode are:
A) All different values  B) All equal to each other  C) Mean is always the highest  D) Mode is always the lowest
**Answer: B)**

**Q19.** In a normal distribution, approximately what percentage of data falls within **1 standard deviation** of the mean?
A) 50%  B) 68%  C) 95%  D) 99.7%
**Answer: B) 68%** — this is the "68-95-99.7 rule": ~68% within 1 SD, ~95% within 2 SD, ~99.7% within 3 SD.

**Q20.** What percentage of data falls within **2 standard deviations** of the mean in a normal distribution?
A) 68%  B) 95%  C) 99.7%  D) 100%
**Answer: B) 95%**

**Q21.** A distribution with a long tail stretching to the right (a few unusually large values) is:
A) Symmetric  B) Negatively skewed  C) Positively skewed  D) Bimodal
**Answer: C) Positively skewed (right-skewed)**

**Q22.** In a positively (right) skewed distribution, which measure is typically pulled to be the **largest**?
A) Mode  B) Median  C) Mean  D) They're always equal
**Answer: C) Mean** — outliers on the right pull the mean upward more than the median.

## E. Correlation, Causation & Hypothesis Testing

**Q23.** "Correlation does not imply causation" means:
A) Two variables can never be related  B) Two variables moving together doesn't prove one causes the other  C) Correlation is always a measurement error  D) Causation always implies correlation, with no exceptions
**Answer: B)**

**Q24.** A correlation coefficient (r) of **+1** indicates:
A) No relationship  B) A perfect negative relationship  C) A perfect positive relationship  D) An unpredictable relationship
**Answer: C)**

**Q25.** A correlation coefficient (r) of **−1** indicates:
A) No relationship  B) A perfect negative relationship  C) A perfect positive relationship  D) Random, unrelated data
**Answer: B)**

**Q26.** A correlation coefficient (r) of **0** indicates:
A) A strong positive relationship  B) A strong negative relationship  C) No linear relationship  D) A perfect relationship
**Answer: C)**

**Q27.** In hypothesis testing, the **null hypothesis** typically represents:
A) The claim you're trying to prove is true  B) The assumption of "no effect" or "no difference"  C) Always the new/alternative theory  D) A biased sample
**Answer: B)**

**Q28.** A small p-value (e.g. p < 0.05) typically suggests:
A) Strong evidence against the null hypothesis  B) Strong evidence for the null hypothesis  C) The sample size was too small to matter  D) The data must be normally distributed
**Answer: A)**

**Q29.** A "random variable" is:
A) A variable whose value is fixed and known  B) A variable whose value depends on the outcome of a random/chance event  C) Always a whole number  D) Only used in algebra problems
**Answer: B)**

**Q30.** Which of these is an example of a **discrete** random variable?
A) The exact height of a student  B) The number of heads in 10 coin tosses  C) The exact weight of a parcel  D) The exact time taken to finish a race
**Answer: B)** — countable whole-number outcomes (0 through 10 heads); the others are continuous measurements.

## Quick-recall summary table (review the night before)

| Concept | One-line takeaway |
|---|---|
| Discrete vs Continuous | Countable vs measurable |
| Nominal / Ordinal / Interval / Ratio | No order → Order, no equal gaps → Equal gaps, no true zero → Equal gaps + true zero |
| Mean vs Median | Mean is outlier-sensitive; median is outlier-resistant |
| Variance / SD | Spread of data; SD = √variance |
| Normal distribution | Symmetric, mean=median=mode, 68-95-99.7 rule |
| Skew | Positive skew → mean is pulled highest; negative skew → mean is pulled lowest |
| Correlation ≠ Causation | Related ≠ one causes the other |
| r = +1 / −1 / 0 | Perfect positive / perfect negative / no linear relationship |
| Null hypothesis | The "no effect" default assumption you try to disprove |
